# Normalcy Bias

 Title: Unraveling the Psychological Puzzle: Normalcy Bias and Its Impact on Decision Making

**Introduction**

Normalcy Bias, a psychological phenomenon, is a cognitive distortion that causes individuals to underestimate or disbelieve potential threats, persisting in believing that things will continue as they always have. This mental model is of significant importance due to its far-reaching implications on our preparedness for disasters and critical situations.

**Core Concept**

The Normalcy Bias arises from the brain's tendency to process new information slowly, taking around 8-10 seconds under normal circumstances. During times of stress, this process is further slowed down, potentially leading to a fixation on default solutions that may or may not be correct. This bias can be traced back to evolutionary survival mechanisms, where staying still during an attack increases the chances of survival by making prey less noticeable to predators (Wikipedia, 2021).

**Examples**

1. **Traffic Collisions**: Given that car crashes are relatively uncommon occurrences for most individuals, Normalcy Bias can lead drivers to underestimate the risk and engage in risky behaviors such as speeding or texting while driving.
2. **Historical Catastrophes**: The sinking of the Titanic serves as a prime example, with passengers and crew failing to evacuate promptly due to their belief that the ship was unsinkable. Similarly, thousands in New Orleans chose to ignore evacuation orders during Hurricane Katrina, underestimating the severity of the impending disaster.
3. **Professional Failures**: In a tragic example from the policing profession, officers have been found to overlook threat cues due to Normalcy Bias, leading to fatal encounters. For instance, an officer might disregard signs of aggression if the situation seems peaceful, leading to instances of violent assault (Wikipedia, 2021).

**Application**

Understanding Normalcy Bias can empower individuals by equipping them with the awareness needed to prepare for potential disasters. By recognizing this cognitive bias, one can actively work towards overcoming it through conscious effort and planning. This includes creating contingency plans, staying informed about potential threats, and practicing quick decision-making in simulated emergency scenarios.

**Related Models**

1. **Optimism Bias**: A related mental model that influences individuals to believe that they are less likely to experience negative events than others (Tversky & Kahneman, 1981).
2. **Black Swan Theory**: A concept describing rare and unpredictable events that have profound impacts on our society (Taleb, 2007). Understanding Normalcy Bias can help individuals prepare for such Black Swan events by recognizing potential threats before they become catastrophic.
