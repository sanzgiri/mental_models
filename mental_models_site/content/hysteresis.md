# Hysteresis

 Title: Hysteresis: A Comprehensive Look at the Dependence of a System on Its History

Hysteresis, derived from the Ancient Greek word for "deficiency" or "lagging behind", is a fascinating mental model that signifies the dependence of a system's state on its history. This concept is crucial because it helps explain the behavior of various systems across physics, chemistry, engineering, biology, and economics, where changes in one variable do not necessarily result in proportional responses, but rather exhibit a lag or persistence.

**Core Concept**

At its core, hysteresis occurs when a system's current state depends on its past states, creating loops or curves that depict different values of one variable based on the direction of change of another. In physics, this is most notably observed in ferromagnetic and ferroelectric materials, where a material may have multiple possible states in a given field, depending on how the field changed previously.

The behavior can be divided into two types: rate-dependent and rate-independent hysteresis. Rate-dependent hysteresis exhibits a dynamic lag between input and output that disappears when the input is varied more slowly. This type of hysteresis is often associated with dissipation, such as power loss in systems subjected to friction. On the other hand, rate-independent hysteresis involves a persistent memory of the past that remains after transients have died out. The future development of such a system depends on the history of states visited but not on the speed at which it traverses the path.

**Examples**

1. Electronic Circuits: Hysteresis is often intentionally added to circuits to prevent unwanted rapid switching, as in Schmitt triggers or latching relays.
2. User Interface Design: In user interface design, hysteresis can be used to smooth out user interactions and make them more intuitive.
3. Aerodynamics: Hysteresis can be observed when decreasing the angle of attack of a wing after stall, regarding the lift and drag coefficients.
4. Elastic Hysteresis: This type of hysteresis is demonstrated using rubber bands and results in energy dissipation due to material internal friction.
5. Contact Angle Hysteresis: In surface science, the contact angle formed between a liquid and solid phase will exhibit a range of contact angles that are possible.
