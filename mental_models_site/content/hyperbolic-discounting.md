# Hyperbolic Discounting

 **Hyperbolic Discounting: A Compelling Mental Model for Time Preference**

Hyperbolic Discounting is a crucial mental model in economics and behavioral finance, offering valuable insights into human decision-making and time preferences. This model, which departs from the conventional exponential discounting, aims to explain why individuals often choose smaller, immediate rewards over larger, delayed ones.

**Core Concept**

Hyperbolic Discounting posits that as delays increase, the value of future rewards decreases at an accelerating rate for shorter periods but slows down for longer ones. This non-linear pattern contrasts with exponential discounting's constant decrease in value per unit delay. Mathematically, Hyperbolic Discounting can be represented as:

    g(D) = 1 / (1 + kD)

where `g(D)` is the discount factor, `D` represents the delay in reward, and `k` is a parameter governing the degree of discounting.

**Examples**

1. **Business Decisions**: Imagine an entrepreneur offered a choice between investing $50,000 today to earn a profit of $100,000 in five years or keeping the initial investment and earning no returns. A hyperbolic discounter might opt for the immediate investment, although they would choose the delayed profit if given the same choice in ten years.
2. **Personal Finance**: Suppose an individual is deciding between paying a $500 credit card bill today or waiting until next month. They might opt to pay later due to the immediate financial strain, even though it results in additional interest charges over time.
3. **Policy Making**: Governments often face trade-offs between short-term political benefits and long-term policy impacts. Hyperbolic Discounting helps explain why they may prioritize short-term gains, even if it means sacrificing more significant, long-term benefits.

**Comparison with Exponential Discounting**

Exponential discounting (f(D) = e^(-kD)) assumes a constant rate of decrease in the value of future rewards for each unit of delay. However, Hyperbolic Discounting shows that this rate decreases as delays become longer.

**Implications and Caution**

Understanding Hyperbolic Discounting can help us navigate our personal and professional lives more effectively. It highlights the importance of considering long-term consequences and avoiding impulsive decisions favoring immediate gratification over long-term benefits.

However, it's crucial to remember that this model is a simplification and may not account for all factors influencing human decision-making. Factors such as risk aversion, cognitive biases, and emotional states can significantly impact our choices, making real-world applications more complex.
