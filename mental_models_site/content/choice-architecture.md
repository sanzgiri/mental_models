# Choice Architecture

 **Summary of Mental Model "Choice Architecture"**

**1. Introduction**

Choice Architecture, coined by Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein in their 2008 book "Nudge", is a mental model that focuses on the design of different ways to present choices to decision-makers and the impact this presentation has on decision-making. By understanding and manipulating these factors, we can influence consumer behavior towards personally and socially desirable actions such as saving for retirement, choosing healthier foods, or registering as an organ donor.

**2. Core Concept**

Choice Architecture revolves around the manipulation of various elements that impact decision-making, including: the number of choices presented, how attributes are described, and the presence of a default option. These techniques, often endorsed by libertarian paternalism, aim to nudge individuals towards optimal decisions while preserving their autonomy and freedom of choice.

**3. Examples**

- **Defaults**: A study on organ donation found that registration rates were twice as high when potential donors had to opt out versus opt in, demonstrating the power of defaults in influencing decision-making. This concept has been applied across various domains, from retirement savings plans to project management.
- **Choice Over Time**: Individuals tend to be myopic, preferring immediate gratification over long-term benefits. By emphasizing future outcomes or limiting time offers, choice architects can help reduce procrastination and encourage responsible decision-making.
- **Partitioning Options and Attributes**: Grouping options or attributes in a particular way can influence consumer choices. For instance, car buyers may be nudged towards more responsible purchases by itemizing practical attributes like gas mileage, safety, and warranty over less important factors like speed, radio, and design.

**4. Application**

In your daily life, you can apply Choice Architecture to make better decisions for yourself or even influence others. For example, when designing a menu at a restaurant, strategically placing healthier options in more prominent locations can nudge customers towards making healthier choices. Similarly, defaulting new employees into retirement savings plans can help them save more for the future without requiring additional effort on their part.

**5. Related Models**

- [Mental Accounting](/models/mental-accounting) - a mental model that explains how individuals organize their financial transactions mentally, leading to biased decision-making.
- [Loss Aversion](/models/loss-aversion) - a concept in behavioral economics that suggests people tend to prefer avoiding losses to acquiring equivalent gains, which plays a significant role in Choice Architecture, especially when it comes to defaults and framing choices.
