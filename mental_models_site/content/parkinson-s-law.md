# Parkinson's Law

 **Introduction**

Parkinson's Law is a fascinating mental model that sheds light on the behavior of work and bureaucracy expansion over time. Coined by naval historian C. Northcote Parkinson in 1955, this model has significant implications for understanding how work habits, administrative structures, and decision-making processes evolve. Understanding Parkinson's Law can help individuals and organizations improve their efficiency, productivity, and ultimately, progress.

**Core Concept**

At its core, Parkinson's Law encompasses two interrelated observations: (1) work expands to fill the time available for its completion, and (2) the number of workers within public administration or bureaucracy tends to grow, regardless of the amount of work to be done. This second observation is more commonly associated with Parkinson's Law and serves as the primary focus of this summary.

To elaborate on the second observation, Parkinson posited that the expansion of bureaucracies can be described by a mathematical equation involving four factors: the number of officials wanting subordinates (k), the number of working hours spent preparing internal memoranda or engaging in micropolitics (m), the difference between the age at hiring and retirement (P), and the number of administrative files actually completed (n). The formula for bureaucratic expansion is x = (2k^m + P) / n, where x represents the annual increase in employees.

**Examples**

1. **Growing Bureaucracy in the British Empire:** Parkinson demonstrated how the number of staff at the Colonial Office grew despite a decline in British colonies to be administered. This growth was attributed to officials seeking subordinates and creating work for each other.
2. **Expansion of the U.S. Department of Defense:** During the Vietnam War, the size of the U.S. Department of Defense increased significantly, while the number of soldiers involved in combat remained relatively constant. The department's expansion was attributed to Parkinson's Law as military officials sought to create more positions and responsibilities for their subordinates.
3. **Increase in Government Agencies:** In many countries, government agencies often experience growth even during periods of economic downturn or decreased demand for services. This growth can be explained by Parkinson's Law as bureaucrats seek to protect their jobs and create more work for each other.

**Application**

To apply Parkinson's Law in daily life, one should be mindful of the tendency for work to expand to fill the available time and for bureaucracies to grow regardless of the amount of work to be done. This awareness can help individuals and organizations identify inefficiencies and take steps to streamline processes, reduce waste, and increase productivity.

1. **Setting Deadlines:** By setting realistic deadlines, one can prevent work from expanding beyond its necessary scope. This approach can help maintain focus and ensure that tasks are completed in a timely manner.
2. **Streamlining Processes:** Identifying unnecessary steps or repetitive tasks within a bureaucratic system can help reduce the expansion of work and decrease the number of employees needed to complete those tasks.
3. **Promoting Collaboration Over Competition:** Encouraging collaboration among colleagues rather than competition for subordinates can help prevent the creation of unnecessary work and promote a more productive working environment.

**Related Models**

1. [The Peter Principle](/models/peter-principle) – This mental model suggests that employees are promoted based on their success in current roles, rather than potential for future success, leading to the "rise to mediocrity."
2. [The Eisenhower Matrix](/models/eisenhower-matrix) – A decision-making tool that helps individuals prioritize tasks based on their urgency and importance, enabling them to maximize productivity and minimize waste.
