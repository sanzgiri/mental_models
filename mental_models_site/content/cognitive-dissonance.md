# Cognitive Dissonance

 Cognitive Dissonance: A Psychological Puzzle of Inconsistent Beliefs and Actions
==============================================================================

In the realm of psychology, the mental model known as **Cognitive Dissonance** serves as a powerful explanation for human behavior in situations where our beliefs, actions, or feelings contradict each other. This theory, first proposed by Leon Festinger in 1957, has been instrumental in understanding various aspects of personal biases and the pursuit of certain behaviors that may seem illogical or inconsistent with one's judgments.

**Core Concept**
----------------

Cognitive Dissonance revolves around the human desire for internal psychological consistency to function effectively in the real world. When our actions, ideas, beliefs, values, or environmental factors clash, we experience a state of discomfort known as cognitive dissonance. To alleviate this discomfort, individuals often change their beliefs, actions, or even seek out information that supports their existing beliefs, rather than challenging them.

**Mechanism**
--------------

The theory suggests that when faced with conflicting cognitions (beliefs, attitudes, or behaviors), people automatically try to resolve the conflict by reframing their beliefs, rationalizing, adopting the just-world fallacy, practicing selective perception, or exhibiting confirmation bias. The magnitude of cognitive dissonance is determined by the importance of the conflicting elements and the ratio of consonant-to-dissonant elements within a person's cognitions.

**Examples**
-------------

1. **Cult members and a failed prophecy**: In Festinger's seminal work, "When Prophecy Fails" (1956), he documented the case of a UFO cult that predicted an alien spacecraft would rescue them from earthly destruction. When the prophecy failed, instead of disbanding, the group members doubled down on their beliefs by adopting environmentalism and social advocacy to save Earth.
2. **Smokers and health risks**: Smokers often exhibit cognitive dissonance when confronted with information about the health risks associated with smoking. To alleviate this discomfort, they may engage in rationalization (e.g., "I'll quit eventually") or selective perception ("Cigarettes don't have that many chemicals").
3. **Elections and partisan bias**: Voters often support candidates who share their political views, even if the candidate's policies are not objectively beneficial. This can be attributed to confirmation bias, where voters seek out information that supports their existing beliefs while ignoring or dismissing contradictory evidence.

**Application**
---------------

Understanding cognitive dissonance can help individuals make more informed decisions by encouraging self-reflection and critical thinking. By recognizing when you might be experiencing cognitive dissonance, you can challenge your assumptions, seek out diverse perspectives, and engage in more objective decision-making processes.

**Selective Exposure**
----------------------

Another important aspect of cognitive dissonance is selective exposure, the tendency for individuals to prefer media that aligns with their current state of mind, mood, or beliefs. This can lead to the reinforcement of existing biases and the avoidance of information that contradicts them.
