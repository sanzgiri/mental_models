# Birth Lottery

 The Birth Lottery, a thought-provoking mental model rooted in philosophy, is a hypothetical scenario where individuals consider principles for society's basic structure without any prior knowledge of their own position within it. This concept, often associated with John Rawls and his book "A Theory of Justice," offers profound insights into fairness and social justice.

In the Birth Lottery, or the Original Position as it is also known, participants are placed behind a metaphorical veil of ignorance. This veil prevents them from knowing their ethnicity, social status, gender, or personal beliefs about a good life. In this state, they are forced to adopt rational decisions that are impartial and promote fairness.

Rawls argues that in this situation, participants would choose two principles of justice:
1. Each citizen is guaranteed a fully adequate scheme of basic liberties, compatible with the same for all others.
2. Social and economic inequalities must benefit the least advantaged as much as possible (the Difference Principle), and be attached to positions open to all.

These principles are designed to create a just and fair society where everyone, regardless of their eventual position, is treated equally. The focus on the least advantaged reflects the risk-averse nature of individuals behind the veil, who make choices to mitigate potential worst-case scenarios.

Here are three real-world examples of the Birth Lottery:
1. **Social Policies**: Governments around the world often implement policies aimed at reducing economic disparities and ensuring basic liberties for all citizens. These policies can be traced back to the principles outlined in the Birth Lottery.
2. **Business Decisions**: Companies that prioritize fairness and equality, such as those with robust equal opportunity hiring practices or minimum wage guarantees, can be seen as applying the Birth Lottery mentality.
3. **Constitutional Drafting**: The creation of constitutions often involves a process similar to the Birth Lottery, where individuals come together to establish principles for the new society that are fair and impartial.

Applying the Birth Lottery in daily life can help individuals make decisions that promote fairness and equality. It encourages us to consider the potential impact of our choices on others, regardless of their circumstances or position in life.

Related mental models that complement the Birth Lottery include:
1. [Counterfactual Thinking](/models/counterfactual-thinking) - a mental model that involves considering alternative outcomes and their implications.
2. [Tabula Rasa](/models/tabula-rasa) - a philosophical concept that suggests people are born without innate ideas, beliefs, or preferences, similar to the veil of ignorance in the Birth Lottery.
